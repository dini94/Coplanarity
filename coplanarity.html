Where the two dihedral angles come from

A typical D–π–A dye has two rotatable junctions: where the donor connects to the π-spacer (φ₁), and where the π-spacer connects to the acceptor (φ₂). Measure each from the DFT-optimized geometry, enter both below, and the tool combines them automatically — this is exactly the two-junction case of the formula in the strip below.

Why cos²(φ), and why geometric mean — the reasoning
Coplanarity vs. planarity. "Coplanarity" is the term DSSC/D–π–A dye papers use specifically for dihedral-angle-based assessments of whether linked donor/spacer/acceptor units share a plane — that's what this tool computes. "Planarity" (Mode C below) is reserved for whole-molecule, atom-position-based metrics. Both terms appear in the broader literature, but this split matches how each is most consistently used.

Why not average the raw angle? A plain average has two bugs: dihedral angles are circular (−178° and +179° describe nearly the same geometry but average to ~0°, not ~179°), and 0°/180° are scored as opposite extremes even though both indicate planarity.

Why cos²(φ) instead. The overlap between two π-systems joined by a single bond scales as cos²(φ) of the dihedral between them — a direct consequence of the p-orbital overlap integral, confirmed experimentally in biphenyl conductance (STM break-junction) and nonlinear-optical studies of torsion-restricted biarylenes. This functional form has already been published as a planarity index, ⟨cos²φ⟩, by Che & Perepichka, and used identically in at least one high-profile conjugated-polymer study. It fixes both bugs above for free: cos²(0°)=cos²(180°)=1, and cosine has no wraparound discontinuity.

Why geometric mean for multiple junctions. No published paper combines several distinct backbone torsions into one number yet, so this part is a reasoned extension rather than a citation. Conjugation must pass through every junction in sequence — donor→spacer, then spacer→acceptor — so one badly-twisted junction should dominate the whole-molecule score — geometric mean does that (a single 90° junction collapses the whole product to 0), whereas an arithmetic mean would just dilute it.

The legacy plinear/pcos values are kept alongside C for transparency, so you can see how the recommendation differs from a simple average.

Score bands for C (coplanarity), grounded in what the number physically means (the fraction of p-orbital overlap retained, since C is itself a cos²-based overlap fraction): Planar C≥0.90 (≥90% of orbital overlap retained, φ≲18° for a single junction — consistent with literature reports that small twists below ~15–20° have minimal effect on conjugation/transmission); Moderate 0.50≤C<0.90 (50–90% retained — this band includes the classic biphenyl twist of ~23°, widely described as "not coplanar"); Twisted C<0.50 (less than half the overlap retained — φ>45° for a single junction, the exact point where cos²φ crosses its own halfway mark). MPP bands (Å) are scaled against a typical aromatic C–C bond length (~1.4 Å): Planar MPP<0.05 Å (<4% of a bond length), Moderate 0.05–0.15 Å, Twisted >0.15 Å. These are reasoned groupings for quick visual scanning, not formally standardized cutoffs — always check the raw number for anything going into a publication.
